# Test 10 (SE Arm) — One-Page Summary

**Task.** Drive the rover straight at a wall (~1 m away) at maximum speed and stop as close as possible without touching it. Two hard constraints: *run at maximum speed* and *no contact*. The arm followed a gated systems-engineering process — analyze, model, calibrate, verify, then five scored operation runs — touching hardware only after the analysis was done.

**Result.** All five operation runs stopped with no contact, **35–36 mm from the wall (mean 35.6, operator-measured), spread ~1 mm** — square, and repeatable to about half a millimeter. The core win was again predict-then-verify: three separate real defects — a wrong drive direction, a 20° veer at full speed, and a badly wrong sensor — were each caught and fixed before any scored run, not by crashing. The cost was six characterization/verification runs (two calibration, four verification) and one operator measurement — more runs than the two-to-three planned, but the measurement held to plan.

**What went wrong, and how it was corrected:**

- **Wrong drive direction (first calibration run), and a slow start that stayed.** The first run drove *away* from the wall: it guessed forward from "which sensor reads shorter," and at ~1 m the forward sensors are noisier than the small test move, so it locked onto the clean rear sensor and called backward "forward." It also over-filled the data link, timed out, and lost its braking data. Fix (second calibration run): identify front vs. rear by absolute standoff, and confirm forward with a longer slow move that must make the *front* pair read *closer*. That slow confirming move — plus a gentle ramp up to speed added later to hold the rover straight — became part of every run, so the rover no longer *begins* at maximum speed.

- **A badly wrong ultrasonic sensor — found only by the one measurement.** With the rover stopped square, the operator measured the true gap: **166 mm**. One forward sensor read 182 mm (accurate — a fixed 16 mm offset); the other read 50 mm, i.e. 116 mm short, because it does not see the flat wall face cleanly up close. So every "close" stop during verification had been read on the bad sensor — the rover was really stopping ~166–200 mm out while it looked like 50–80. It was dealt with decisively: the bad sensor was **dropped entirely** — from control *and* from the emergency-stop logic, since a false-low reading would trip a needless stop — and control moved to the good sensor (a standstill anchor) plus the wheel encoders. The retrospective's headline lesson: a sensor can be perfectly *repeatable* and completely *wrong*; ground-truth every important channel *before* building on it, not after.

- **A 20° veer at full speed, and the campaign's first closed-loop steering.** The first verification run drove straight open-loop and veered ~20°, stopping badly skewed — a clear straightness failure. Cause: the two motors aren't matched, and the mismatch only shows at full throttle. The first steering fix did *nothing*, because at full command both motors are already pinned at their physical maximum — you can't speed a wheel past its limit to steer. The working fix backed cruise down to ~97% of maximum (below that limit) and closed a heading loop that gently slows the outside wheel, with a ramped launch to kill the start-up transient. Heading then held to ~3° and the stop came in square.

- **Telemetry as a real constraint.** The hub's data link drains only ~6 lines per second; the first run pushed far more, blew its timeout, and truncated the braking data it most needed. The fix was to treat telemetry as a budgeted resource — downsample, keep it off the fast control loop, always end with a flush marker — after which runs completed cleanly.

- **The four verification runs, in sequence.** Each bought a specific piece. Run 1 exposed the 20° veer. Run 2 proved the naïve steering fix was a no-op (motors saturated) and diagnosed why. Run 3 finally drove straight and square — which made the 166 mm measurement a clean perpendicular gap, and that measurement is what exposed the bad sensor. Run 4 verified the rebuilt encoder-based control against a frozen prediction and passed everything (true gap 36.7 mm vs a frozen ~40; the two good channels agreeing to 5.7 mm; heading 1.6°). Three of four were spent finding and fixing the veer and the sensor; only the last confirmed the final design.

**Stopping-algorithm changes (top level).** Two versions of *what decides the stop*. (1) Trigger on the forward ultrasonic reading, full-speed open-loop, motor brake — abandoned once that sensor proved unreliable near the wall and the open-loop drive veered 20°. (2) Trigger on **wheel-encoder travel measured from a standstill sensor anchor**, with active heading steering throughout — encoder and anchor are both lag-free, and the bad in-motion sensor is excluded from control *and* safety. The stop command itself (a motor brake, ~13 mm of roll after the trigger) never changed — only which signals decide the stop, plus the added steering.

**What held up, and what was luck.** The tight repeatability was *earned* — it came from steering the rover square every run and stopping on validated, lag-free channels, not a sensor that happened to work. The one soft spot: the wheel-encoder estimate ran a **consistent 3–4 mm high**, so the rover stopped a few millimeters *closer* to the wall than its own estimate every run — predictable and covered by the margin, but it is the toward-the-wall direction and only became visible across all five runs. And the single measurement was spent *late* — after three runs had already optimized against the bad sensor; spent first, as a 60-second standoff check, it would have caught that sensor immediately and roughly halved the run count. That, not luck, is where the run budget went.

**Score against the criteria.**
1. Characterization/verification runs (fewer better): **6** — **2 calibration** (one failed on direction + truncation, one clean) + **4 verification** (veer found; steering no-op diagnosed; straight + anchor; final confirm) — vs a 2-to-3-run plan.
2. Human interventions (fewer better): **1** — the single true-gap measurement (166 mm), held to plan. The five close-out ground-truth gaps are scoring, not interventions.
3. Operation runs with no contact (more better): **5 / 5**.
4. Closeness of stops: **35–36 mm, mean 35.6, spread ~1 mm, standard deviation ≈ 0.5 mm.**
5. Predicted vs actual error: **35.6 mm − 34 mm = +1.6 mm** — 1.6 mm farther than the committed ~34 mm operation prediction (tightened from VER-4's verified ~40 mm), i.e. a hair more clearance; the operator-calibrated onboard channel tracked the true gap to within a few mm, mean error under 1 mm.
