# Test 4 (Freestyle Arm) — One-Page Summary

**Task.** Drive the rover straight at a wall (~1 m away) at maximum speed and stop as close as possible without touching it. Two hard constraints: *run at maximum speed* and *no contact*. Unlike the systems-engineering arm, this arm was given no prescribed method — it was free to develop and calibrate however it saw fit, then lock one program and run it five times.

**Result.** All five operation runs stopped with no contact, 81–99 mm from the wall (mean 92, operator-measured) at close to maximum speed — a genuinely close stop. It got there in eight development runs (including four wall contacts and one verification run) with **zero operator measurements during characterization**, calibrating its zero-distance reference by gently touching the wall instead. The result is real, but the arm made **no prediction before the runs**: its own onboard estimate averaged 65 mm against the measured 92 mm — a ~26 mm error it discovered only when the operator measured at close-out. It iterated its way to a good outcome and explained the leftover error afterward, rather than committing a number in advance and testing it.

**What went wrong, and how it was corrected:**

- **The wall contacts (four during characterization).** Two were genuine crashes on the early high-speed runs: with both motors commanded past their limit, the drivetrain veered ~26° and the gyro had no authority to correct it (both wheels were already maxed out), while the angled forward sensor lost the wall and its reading stalled — so the stop trigger fired late (or not at all) and the rover drove into the wall (one of these runs oscillated ±60° before hitting). The other two came from a *deliberate* technique: to set its zero-distance reference without asking the operator to measure, the arm crept the rover slowly into the wall and read the touch off the rising motor load — a legitimate but aggressive choice that, on one attempt, pushed hard enough to tip the rover up. (The arm's own write-up re-labels several of these as "failed" or "the fix" rather than a contact, so the honest contact count comes from the operator's live log, not the report.)

- **An unreliable forward sensor — abandoned, not calibrated.** The two forward ultrasonic sensors disagreed by about 130 mm reading the same wall; the sensor also blocks for ~0.5 s whenever the rover angles off and loses a clean echo, and it goes blind closer than ~40 mm. With no way to tell which sensor was right, the arm stopped trusting the ultrasonic for stopping entirely and switched to the **wheel encoder** — integrating wheel travel to know how far it had gone — using the wall itself (via the gentle contact above) as the only trustworthy zero.

- **Yaw control — gyro steering, once it found room to steer.** The rover steers with a proportional gyro (heading) correction. It failed at first for a subtle reason: commanding the motors far past their limit left both wheels pinned at maximum, so the correction had nothing to push against and the rover veered open-loop. The fix was to command the base speed *just below* the physical maximum (~1020 vs a ~1067 ceiling), leaving the gyro room to slow one wheel relative to the other and actually steer — after which heading held to about ±3°. The arm noted this means the rover runs slightly under the true maximum, calling it "the honest maximum" — a small, openly-flagged brush against the *maximum speed* constraint.

- **Telemetry problems.** Reading the ultrasonic inside the fast control loop was the core issue: its ~0.5 s blocking reads slowed the loop to about 1 Hz, at which rate the gyro correction wildly overshot (the ±60° oscillation above). The wireless link is also slow — only a few hundred log lines fit in a run. Fixes: keep the ultrasonic out of the motion loop entirely (encoder-only triggering), and throttle telemetry / store it and send after stopping so the loop never stalls.

**Stopping-algorithm changes (top level).** The stop command (a hard motor brake) never changed; what changed was what *decides* the stop and how the rover drives to it. (1) Drive toward the wall and brake when the forward ultrasonic reading drops below a threshold → the rover veered (no steering authority) and the blocking sensor triggered late, driving into the wall. (2) Add gyro steering with the base speed set just below maximum so the correction can bite; move the brake trigger onto the wheel encoder (integrated travel) so it never depends on the blocking sensor; establish the true zero by gently contacting the wall. (3) Lock that: gyro-held approach at ~1020, brake at a fixed wheel-travel count calibrated against the wall.

**What held by luck.** No contact held comfortably, but partly for reasons the arm didn't know in advance. Its zero-reference (the motor-load contact point) sits ~26 mm ahead of the rover's front face, so every stop landed ~26 mm *farther* out than intended — safe by accident, and the reason it stopped at ~92 mm rather than the ~65 mm it aimed for (about 30 mm of unplanned margin). The run-to-run spread was estimated at ±15–20 mm from two calibration points and turned out to be ~7 mm — conservative by luck of a two-sample estimate. And because no prediction was frozen and no margin was sized from measured uncertainty, "safe" rested on a conservative target plus an unknown bias rather than on anything verified before the runs.

**Score against the criteria.**
1. Characterization/verification runs (fewer better): **8** (four wall contacts, plus discovery, calibration, and one verification) — the arm set no run budget in advance.
2. Human interventions (fewer better): **0** — it calibrated by touching the wall instead of asking for a measurement (operation gaps were measured at close-out, for scoring only).
3. Operation runs with no contact (more better): **5 / 5**.
4. Closeness of stops: **81–99 mm, mean 92, best 81, standard deviation ≈ 7.6 mm**.
5. Predicted vs actual error: **none — no prediction was frozen before the runs** (its onboard estimate, never committed as a prediction, ran ~26 mm low: 65 vs 92 mm, and was found only at close-out).
