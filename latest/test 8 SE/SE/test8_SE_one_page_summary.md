# Test 8 (SE Arm) — One-Page Summary

**Task.** Drive the rover straight at a wall (~1 m away) at maximum speed and stop as close as possible without touching it. Two hard constraints: *run at maximum speed* and *no contact*. The arm followed a gated systems-engineering process — analyze, model, calibrate, verify, then five scored operation runs — touching hardware only after the analysis was done.

**Result.** All five operation runs stopped with no contact, 31–50 mm from the wall (mean 43.4 mm, operator-measured), at full speed. The core win was predict-then-verify: before any scored run the arm froze a written prediction of the stopping gap (45 mm), and the five runs came in at a measured mean of 43.4 mm — within 1.6 mm of that frozen number, all five inside the predicted spread. The rover's own onboard gap estimate agreed with the operator's tape measurements to ±2.6 mm, so the single human measurement used to calibrate the sensor held across every run. No defect shipped. Getting there took five characterization/verification runs (against a three-run plan) and one human measurement, because several problems with the *platform and instrumentation* — not the physics — had to be found and fixed once the rover started moving.

**What went wrong, and how it was corrected:**

- **A start-of-run discovery move corrupted the first run.** To work out which motors and sensors faced forward, the first program spun the wheels in short test pulses. One pulse rotated the whole rover about 18°, and the following pulse didn't undo it, so the fast approach started crooked and drifted further (ending ~33° off). A crooked approach makes the distance sensors read the wall at an angle instead of straight on, so the stopping-distance data from that run was unusable. Fix: the motor/sensor layout was locked in from what the first run *had* successfully identified, and all later runs skipped the test pulses and drove straight from a squared start.

- **The drivetrain veers on its own — active steering was added.** Even with both wheels commanded equally, the rover pulled ~14° to one side at full speed (a motor imbalance). Driving open-loop couldn't hold a straight line, and the veer corrupts the gap reading. Fix: closed-loop steering using the onboard gyro — a fixed bias to cancel the known imbalance plus a proportional-and-derivative correction on measured heading — which kept the approach straight to within about ±4°. This was tuned by trial on hardware (a controls problem solved on scored runs rather than offline, which cost a run).

- **A faulty distance sensor was found and dropped.** The rover carries two forward ultrasonic sensors as a cross-check. They disagreed by ~124 mm — a gap that at first looked like a side effect of the crooked approach. Once the approach was straightened and the ~124 mm offset persisted, and one sensor also intermittently latched and dropped out, that sensor was diagnosed as faulty (a flat wall can't produce a 124 mm difference) and excluded. The good sensor was kept as the sole ranger, and the single operator gap measurement later confirmed it read correctly.

- **The wireless data link couldn't keep up.** Telemetry off the hub runs at only ~35 lines per second, and the early runs sent so much data that the log was cut off before the rover's final resting distance came through — the exact number needed to measure stopping distance. An attempt to speed things up by bundling all the data into one big write ran about 5× *slower* and was reverted. Fix: send one line at a time, send the resting-distance rows first, thin out the logged channels, and allow a longer window; scored runs log only a short summary.

**Stopping-algorithm changes (top level).** The command that actually stops the rover — a passive motor brake — never changed; what changed was *what decides* the stop and *which signals it trusts*. Three versions: (1) discover the sensors on the fly, then brake when either forward sensor dropped below a cautious distance → the discovery move spun the rover and corrupted the run; (2) fixed sensor layout, drive straight from a squared start, still brake on the ultrasonic below a cautious distance, plus added gyro-based steering to hold the line and fixed the telemetry; (3) after the faulty sensor was dropped, brake on the single good sensor below the calibrated operating distance, with that sensor's offset to the rover's front anchored by the one operator measurement. The stopping distance itself was *measured* directly at full speed rather than modeled, since the rover only ever runs at one speed.

**What held by luck.** Two things were safe by good fortune rather than by proof. First, the safety cushion was sized from only one or two clean stops — the arm *guessed* the run-to-run spread at ~12 mm; the real spread turned out tighter (~7.7 mm), so the cushion was conservative by luck of a small sample, not because the spread was known before the runs. Second, the sensor's offset to the rover's front was measured once at a *far* distance (~540 mm) but then used at the *near-wall* stop (~60 mm — right at the sensor's short-range limit). Whether that offset still held at close range was never independently checked before the scored runs; it happened to hold (confirmed only afterward, to within a few mm). Had it drifted at close range, nothing in the verification step would have caught it — the check compared the rover's estimate against a prediction built from the same sensor.

**Score against the criteria.**
1. Characterization/verification runs (fewer better): **5** — one discovery run (corrupted by the spin), three straight-approach re-runs forced by the veer and the telemetry limit, and the verification run — vs a 3-run plan.
2. Human interventions (fewer better): **1** — the single operator gap measurement (542 mm) that anchored the sensor-to-front offset — vs a 1-measurement plan. (The five close-out gap measurements are the scoring itself, not inputs the arm requested.)
3. Operation runs with no contact (more better): **5 / 5**.
4. Closeness of stops: **31–50 mm, mean 43.4, best 31, standard deviation 7.7 mm**.
5. Predicted vs actual error: **43.4 mm − 45 mm = −1.6 mm** (landed 1.6 mm closer than the frozen prediction; all five within ~1.2 standard deviations of it).
