# Test 7 (Freestyle Arm) — One-Page Summary

**Task.** Drive the rover straight at a wall (~1 m away) at maximum speed and stop as close as possible without touching it. Two hard constraints: *run at maximum speed* and *no contact*. Unlike the systems-engineering arm, this arm was given no prescribed method — free to develop and calibrate however it judged best, then lock one program and run it five times.

**Result.** All five operation runs stopped with no contact, **153–185 mm** from the wall (mean **175 mm**) at full speed. It got there in **five characterization runs** — one of which contacted the wall, and one of which it aborted through its own telemetry bug — with **no discretionary operator measurements**, relying on the sensor's physical floor to guarantee no-contact. The runs were real and highly repeatable, but the arm committed **no prediction it tested in advance**, and its onboard gap estimate averaged ~45 mm against the measured ~175 mm — a ~130 mm error it discovered only at the close-out.

**What went wrong, and how it was corrected:**

- **The wall contact (one characterization run).** To characterize contact, the arm crept the rover slowly into the wall, planning to detect the touch by the wheels stalling. Instead the wheels *slipped* while the bumper was pinned, so its detector never fired and the rover ground against the wall until timeout. It got no clean contact reading, but learned the sensor floors out at contact — which it later leaned on as a conservative no-contact guarantee.

- **A faulty forward sensor — mistaken for mounting geometry, and used anyway.** The two forward ultrasonic sensors disagreed by ~133 mm on the same wall. Rather than suspect a fault, the arm attributed this to their mounting positions, chose the *closer-reading* sensor (B) as its primary channel, and declined the one ground-truth measurement that would have pinned the absolute offset — judging the sensor's floor enough for safety. That was the pivotal error. Sensor B actually reads ~125 mm *short* of true and cannot report below 40 mm, going blind below ~165 mm of true gap, so a sensor-based stop can't safely get closer than ~165 mm — which is why the stops sat at ~175 mm, far out. The cap was self-inflicted: the *other* sensor (A) reads close to true and would have allowed a far closer stop, exactly what the systems-engineering arm achieved on this same rover (32–51 mm) by diagnosing the same fault and switching to A. The arm's own retrospective still concluded the sensor — not its method — was the limit, and recommended one early ground-truth measurement next time.

- **Yaw — left open-loop, uncorrected.** The arm considered active steering but drove open-loop: both motors at equal speed, heading only *logged*. The rover drifted ~10° by the wall and ~16° through braking — well past the systems-engineering arm's drift — but it judged this acceptable (a flat wall makes forward distance what matters) and let the no-contact margin absorb it. This kept both wheels at true maximum, honoring the maximum-speed constraint cleanly with no slowdown for steering — but it only stayed safe because the stop was so far out; at a tight gap, 16° of drift would have risked a corner contact.

- **Telemetry challenges — a recurring theme.** Reading two ultrasonics per loop was slow (~56 ms), and emitting every loop filled the wireless buffer and stretched some iterations to ~180 ms — ~84 mm of travel between reads at max speed, making the trigger jittery. Fix: buffer in memory during the approach, send after braking. But that caused the next failure — with no per-loop send the loop spun at ~1 ms (the sensor returns a cached value instantly), piling up thousands of samples whose dump overran the timeout and *aborted a validation run* before any stopping data returned. Second fix: throttle the buffer and emit the key readings *before* the dump. Backpressure also corrupted an early coast measurement (175 mm, later found to be a stale-reading artifact and discarded; true coast ~50 mm).

**Stopping-algorithm changes (top level).** The stop command — a hard passive **motor brake** on both motors — never changed; what changed was what *decides* the brake. (1) Drive open-loop and brake when the forward sensor reads below a threshold, then coast ~50 mm to rest. (2) Keep the trigger loop tight by buffering telemetry, so a slow link can't fire the brake late. (3) After a validation run revealed the sensor intermittently *freezes* (returns a stale value), add **odometry-bridged** triggering — the sensor stays primary, but between fresh readings the distance is extrapolated from wheel travel, and the brake fires when that bridged estimate reaches ~110 mm — plus a dead-reckoning failsafe if the wheels roll far enough that the rover should be near the wall but the sensor never triggered. A deliberate safety property runs through all of it: every error mode (freeze, glitch, dropout, slip) pushes the estimate *down*, so the rover always brakes *earlier* and stops *farther* — nothing makes it brake late.

**What held by luck.** No-contact was robust against *dynamic* errors by design — but the safe outcome still rested on luck. The sensor's large offset happened to point the *safe* way (reading short, so the rover stops farther out). The arm couldn't verify the direction and actually assumed the *opposite* — that the sensor read slightly long. Had it read 125 mm *long* instead, the identical logic would have driven straight into the wall. And the uncontrolled ~16° drift never caused a corner contact only because the stop was so far out. "Safe" rested on an unverified offset direction and a large gap, not on anything confirmed before the runs.

**Score against the criteria.**
1. Characterization/verification runs (fewer better): **5** (one wall contact during contact calibration, one aborted by the arm's own telemetry bug) — no run budget set in advance.
2. Human interventions (fewer better): **0** — the arm took no discretionary measurements during characterization, relying on the 40 mm sensor floor for its no-contact guarantee (operation gaps were measured at close-out, for scoring only).
3. Operation runs with no contact (more better): **5 / 5**.
4. Closeness of stops: **153–185 mm, mean 175, best 153, sample standard deviation ≈ 13.0 mm**.
5. Predicted vs actual error: **none — no prediction was frozen and tested before the runs**; the uncommitted onboard estimate would have scored **true − estimate ≈ 175 − 45 = +130 mm** (ran low — rover farther than it believed), found only at close-out.
