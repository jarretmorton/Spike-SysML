# Test 6 (Freestyle Arm) — One-Page Summary

**Task.** Drive the rover straight at a wall (~1 m away) at maximum speed and stop as close as possible without touching it. Two hard constraints: *run at maximum speed* and *no contact*. Unlike the systems-engineering arm, this arm was given no prescribed method — it was free to develop and calibrate however it saw fit, then lock one program and run it five times.

**Result.** All five operation runs stopped with no contact at full speed (~510 mm/s), but the five split cleanly in two: the **first two landed on target at 39 and 49 mm** (mean 44), while the **last three stopped ~200 mm short, at 239–262 mm** — caused by a forward sensor that read the *starting* distance ~200 mm low on those runs while looking perfectly steady, with the whole stop anchored to that one reading. It got there in six characterization runs and one ground-truth measurement, and built a genuinely sound stopping method (counting wheel travel, not trusting the sensor up close). The catch: the onboard estimate read ~44 mm on *every* run — right on the two good runs, ~200 mm wrong on the three bad ones, with no internal warning.

**What went wrong, and how it was corrected:**

- **The faulty ultrasonic sensor — found by one measurement, then designed around.** A single ground-truth reading during characterization (bumper measured at 224 mm while sensor A read 285 and sensor B read 156) exposed the sensors as unreliable: **sensor B** reads low and turns to garbage closer than ~180 mm, and **sensor A**, though steady at ~1 m, reads progressively low near the wall and occasionally carries a large fixed bias. The fix was to make the **wheel encoder** the only trusted channel for the stop, using the sensor only as a coarse start reference — the single reading that later broke three runs.

- **Why the five runs split into two families.** All five ran the same method — measure the start distance once, then brake on counted wheel travel — so the split came entirely from that one reading. On the first two runs it was valid (~1025 mm), so they braked as intended and landed at 39 and 49 mm; on the last three the sensor read the ~970 mm start ~200 mm low (815–833 mm) while looking rock-steady, so the rover braked ~200 mm early and stopped at 239–262 mm. Back-computing each start (measured gap + wheel travel) gives a consistent ~970 mm every run — the odometry was accurate throughout; only the absolute start reference failed.

- **The backward calibration run.** The rover's distance-per-wheel-degree figure kept coming out inconsistent (0.448, 0.529, 0.467) — every forward measurement was contaminated by the flaky sensor or wheel slip. The fix was a run driven *away* from the wall at full speed (capped short of the start line, zero contact risk): sensor A stays in its reliable far range, so its clean before/after reading against exact wheel rotation gave a trustworthy calibration (0.484 mm/degree) that also captured high-speed slip.

- **Yaw control — none, and it didn't need it.** The rover never steers — it drives both wheels at full command and only logs heading. Because the motors are mirror-mounted and well-matched, tracking was naturally good (net drift only −1° to −6° over a full-speed run), so the arm added no correction. It worked, but it's uncorrected: it held because the drivetrain happened to track straight, not because anything kept it straight.

- **Telemetry — the wireless link was the bottleneck.** The Bluetooth radio sustains only ~5 log lines per second. Early on this stalled the rover — one characterization run hit its time cap just streaming ~110 buffered lines after stopping. The fix: buffer readings during motion and stream them only after the rover stops, downsample the log, and emit the summary numbers first (and twice) so they survive a cut-off mid-dump.

**Stopping-algorithm changes (top level).** The stop *command* — a hard motor brake — never changed (it adds only ~13–15 mm and doesn't lunge, which is why it was picked over a coasting stop). What changed was what *decides* the stop. (1) The first idea, braking when the live forward sensor reading crossed a threshold, was rejected: the sensor lags ~80 ms, steps in 40–90 mm jumps, and reads garbage up close, all adding large timing jitter. (2) The adopted method triggers the brake purely on **counted wheel travel** (distance-per-degree × rotation) to leave the intended gap; safety backstops (an over-travel limit, a wheel-stall contact detector, a time cap) can only make it stop *earlier*, and there is no close-range sensor floor.

**What held by luck.** The three short runs erred in the *safe* direction (farther out), so no contact — the same-size error toward the wall would have hit it, and nothing in the design guaranteed the sign. And the fault was uncatchable from onboard data: a constant start-reading offset cancels out of any slope or rate check, so no within-run consistency test could have flagged it. Separately, the wheel-slip spread was judged from only a couple of runs, so precision rested partly on a small-sample estimate.

**Score against the criteria.**
1. Characterization runs (fewer better): **6** — discovery, direction, high-speed brake, encoder validation, backward calibration, and forward validation (one further flash failed to connect and never ran, so it isn't counted).
2. Human interventions (fewer better): **1** — a single front-bumper ground-truth measurement during characterization (the five end-of-run measurements are the mandated close-out reconciliation, not mid-task input).
3. Operation runs with no contact (more better): **5 / 5**.
4. Closeness of stops: **39 and 49 mm on the two runs with a valid start reading (mean 44, best 39); 239–262 mm on the three corrupted runs (mean ~250); sample standard deviation ≈ 113 mm across all five.** That 113 mm reflects the two-family split from the injected sensor fault, not run-to-run precision.
5. Predicted vs actual error: **none — no prediction was frozen before the runs**; the uncommitted onboard estimate would have scored **true − estimate = 167 − 44 = +123 mm** against the five-run average (ran low — rover farther than it believed), found only at close-out.
