# Test 6 (SE Arm) — One-Page Summary

**Task.** Drive the rover straight at a wall (~1 m away) at maximum speed and stop as close as possible without touching it. Two hard constraints: *run at maximum speed* and *no contact*. The arm followed a gated systems-engineering process — analyze, model, calibrate, verify, then five scored operation runs — touching hardware only after the analysis was done.

**Result.** All five operation runs stopped with no contact, at full speed — but well short of the objective: the rover came to rest 135–157 mm from the wall (mean ~148, operator-measured) while it believed it was stopping ~40–50 mm out. The cause was a forward-sensor reading that ran about 97 mm short of the true gap the entire time, which the design never caught within the scored runs. The same bias contaminated the frozen prediction: before the runs the arm froze, at Gate C, a predicted true rest gap of 44 mm, but anchored to a δ=0 assumption that carried that same short reading. Those five true gaps — supplied post-hoc after scoring — are what finally exposed it. So on a like-for-like true-gap basis the error is +104 mm, but net of the ~97 mm bias the prediction held to ~7 mm: a measurement-validation miss, not a prediction miss, and not comparable to the other tests' errors. Getting there took five characterization/verification runs and three human interventions, more than the two-run, one-measurement plan. No contact was solid; closeness was the miss.

**What went wrong, and how it was corrected:**

- **It stopped far because a ranging fault was mis-diagnosed as harmless.** On the first run the two forward sensors read ~133 mm apart at a squared start, both steady (so not noise). Cross-checking the channels flagged it — the detection worked — but the arm read it as a benign mounting offset (one sensor simply sitting farther forward) rather than a ranging fault, and concluded the triggering sensor read the true gap. That zero-offset conclusion rested on one ground-truth measurement taken far from the wall and referenced to the chassis — not the sensor, at the close range where it actually triggers — and the closeness objective was only ever checked against the same sensor that drove the stop, a circular check with no independent reference. The stop then fired on a reading ~97 mm short on every run, so the rover stopped ~100 mm farther out than it thought, and the bias stayed hidden until the operator measured the five gaps at close-out. Not corrected within the scored runs; characterized afterward, with a fix proposed for a future attempt.

- **The stop command set what the wheel encoder could be trusted for.** The stop used an aggressive motor brake, chosen for the sharpest, most repeatable stop. But braking locks the wheels and they skid, so the encoder under-counts the stopping distance — it freezes at the stop while the rover slides ~50 mm. The arm learned to trust the encoder only for rolling travel and take the stopping distance from the ultrasonic and the operator measurement instead.

- **Telemetry overran near the timeout.** The full-resolution stream over Bluetooth was too slow — the first run's dump ran past the host time cap and cut off the end-of-run marker, and a later dump was truncated. Fix: compute the headline numbers on the hub and send them as single values, with no bulk buffer, so the dump was instant.

- **No closed loop on yaw — a deliberate choice.** The rover never steers; it drives both wheels equally and logs heading but never corrects it. A trim would have to slow the leading wheel below maximum, brushing the max-speed constraint, and the arm argued yaw wasn't a contact risk because the front-most point is the sensor itself. Drift stayed within a few degrees on the scored runs but reached ~15° on one calibration run, so staying in bounds during operation was not guaranteed by design.

- **Two verification runs.** A verification run tests the frozen prediction of where the rover will stop before the scored runs — here ~41 mm. The first was falsified: close to the wall the ultrasonic is unreliable, and the onboard resting reading came back physically impossible (farther from the wall than where the rover had braked). The arm rejected it, re-derived the plan (predicted gap now ~44 mm, operator measurement made authoritative that close), and ran a second verification run, which stopped with no contact as predicted.

**Stopping-algorithm changes.** The stop *command* never changed — an aggressive motor brake throughout. What changed was setup and data volume. Version 1 auto-discovered ports and drive directions with a start-of-run motor "nudge," then triggered when the nearer forward sensor crossed a threshold; on this drivetrain the nudge spun the rover ~28°, so it ran off-square and the data was thrown out. Version 2 hard-coded the discovered ports and directions (removing the spin), drove straight from the squared start, kept the same forward-sensor trigger with a last-valid-reading fallback, and used lean on-hub telemetry. The trigger stayed on the forward ultrasonic the whole way — and that reading's hidden ~97 mm bias is what cost the closeness.

**What held by luck.** No contact itself was robust, not lucky — the stop fires in the sensor's reliable range and the physics leaves no way to reach the wall. But the comfortable ~148 mm margin was inflated by the same bias that hurt closeness: the ranging error happened to point *away* from the wall, so the rover stopped short and safe; the identical error pointing the other way could have caused contact. The run-to-run spread was also sized from only two calibration stops.

**Score against the criteria.**
1. Characterization/verification runs (fewer better): **5** — three calibration (the first invalidated by an in-place spin) plus two verification — against a two-run plan.
2. Human interventions (fewer better): **3** — the operator's ground-truth gap measurement, a squared-to-the-wall confirmation batched into that same first measurement, and a no-contact confirmation at the verification run — against a one-measurement plan.
3. Operation runs with no contact (more better): **5 / 5**.
4. Closeness of stops: **135–157 mm, mean ~148, best 135, standard deviation ≈ 9 mm**.
5. Predicted vs actual error: **148 mm − 44 mm = +104 mm** — dominated by the undetected ~97 mm sensor bias; net of it the prediction held to ~7 mm (a measurement-validation miss, not comparable to the other tests — see Result).
